8.4 Sample Size and Power Analysis

In the previous sections, we explored effect sizes, alpha, and power. In this section, we turn to sample size (N) and bring everything together through power analysis.

What is sample size?

Sample size is the total number of participants in a study (often denoted as n). In between-subjects designs, we also consider how many participants are in each group, especially if group sizes are unequal.

How do we determine what sample size I need?

There are several ways researchers determine sample size:

  1. Resource constraints: Time, budget, and access to participants may limit sample size
  2. Accuracy or a priori power analysis: Based on desired power and expected effect size
  3. Heuristics: Rules of thumb from prior literature (generally discouraged)
Note

This information comes from Daniel Lakens’ paper and chapter out on the topic and his sample size justification online tool.

The first option is more of a research methods issue and will not be discussed in this statistics textbook. The third option should be avoided whenever possible.

This leads us to the most important approach:

Conducting an a priori power analysis

We’ll cover it broadly here, but if you want to learn more about conducting power analyses in jamovi Bartlett & Charles (2022) have a great preprint on the topic.

BEAN: Power analysis

We previously saw how alpha and power relate to one another. Through interactive tools, you may have also noticed that effect size and sample size relate to alpha and power.

This is the core idea of BEAN:

If you know three components of BEAN, you can determine the fourth.

Power, effect size, alpha, and sample size all interrelate. Typically, we are trying to answer one of three questions:

  1. What sample size do I need given effect size, alpha, and power?
  2. What power do I have given effect size, alpha, and sample size?
  3. What effect size can I detect given alpha, power, and sample size?

Tools for power analysis

There are several tools available for conducting power analyses:

  • G*Power is perhaps most widely used; there are various guides on how to use G*Power, including Erdfelder et al. (1996) and Faul et al., (2007)
  • Specialized calculators for specific analyses
  • Simulation-based approaches (more advanced)
Note

If you’d like to learn more about power analyses in a variety of experimental designs using G*Power or R packages, I recommend this article by Perugini et al. (2018) called “A practical primer to power analysis for simple experimental designs.”

For this course, we will use the PAMLj module in jamovi, which simplifies these calculations.

Using PAMLj for power analysis in jamovi

Instead of jpower, we will use the PAMLj module in jamovi for power analysis. PAMLj provides a flexible and user-friendly way to conduct power analyses across a range of designs and statistical tests.

What PAMLj can do

PAMLj allows you to conduct power analyses by specifying different combinations of BEAN components.

You can use it to:

  • Calculate required sample size (N)
  • Estimate power for a given design
  • Determine what effect size is detectable

These directly correspond to the three core BEAN questions:

  1. What sample size do I need?
  2. What power do I have?
  3. What effect size can I detect?

PAMLj operationalizes BEAN—it allows you to solve for one component given the others.

How to use PAMLj in jamovi

To conduct a power analysis:

  1. Open jamovi
  2. Install and load the PAMLj module (from the Modules library)
  3. Navigate to the power analysis section
  4. Choose your statistical test (e.g., independent samples t-test)

Then, you will:

  • Select what you want to solve for (N, power, or effect size)
  • Enter the remaining BEAN components
  • Specify:
    • One-tailed vs two-tailed test
    • Group sizes (if applicable)

Applying PAMLj to BEAN questions

1. What sample size do I need?

This is the most common use of power analysis. To determine required sample size:

  • Choose Solve for N
  • Enter:
    • Expected effect size (from Section 8.1)
    • Desired power (e.g., 80% or higher)
    • Alpha (e.g., .05)

PAMLj will return the required sample size per group.

Interpretation:

  • Smaller effect size → larger required sample
  • Higher desired power → larger required sample
  • Lower alpha → larger required sample

Sample size is the lever we adjust to achieve sufficient power.

2. What power do I have?

Sometimes, sample size is fixed due to constraints. To determine power:

  • Choose Solve for power
  • Enter:
    • Sample size
    • Effect size
    • Alpha

PAMLj will estimate your power.

Interpretation:

  • Low power → high risk of missing real effects
  • High power → greater likelihood of detecting effects

This helps us evaluate whether a study is capable of detecting meaningful effects.

3. What effect size can I detect?

This question is especially useful when planning studies with limited resources. To determine detectable effect size:

  • Choose Solve for effect size
  • Enter:
    • Sample size
    • Desired power
    • Alpha

PAMLj will estimate the smallest effect size you can reliably detect.

Interpretation:

  • Small sample → only large effects detectable
  • Large sample → smaller effects detectable

This connects directly back to Section 8.1: what effect size do we care about?

Example: Applying BEAN with PAMLj

Let’s revisit our earlier example:

  • Effect size: d = .8
  • Alpha: .05
  • Sample size: 25 per group
  • Two-tailed test

Using PAMLj, we estimate:

  • Power ≈ 79%

This tells us:

We have about a 79% chance of detecting an effect of this size.

Try it yourself

Use PAMLj to explore:

  • What happens to sample size when you increase power?
  • What happens to power when you decrease effect size?
  • What happens when you lower alpha?

These explorations help you build intuition about how BEAN works in practice.

TipCheck Your Understanding
  1. What are the three main questions PAMLj helps answer?
  2. What happens to required sample size when desired power increases?
  3. What happens to detectable effect size when sample size increases?
  4. Why is power analysis considered the practical application of BEAN?
TipAnswers
  1. Sample size, power, and detectable effect size
  2. It increases
  3. Smaller effects become detectable
  4. Because it uses all BEAN components together to guide study design

Bringing BEAN together: Making research decisions

By this point, you have seen each component of BEAN individually:

  • Effect size → what we care about
  • Alpha → how strict we are about evidence
  • Power → our ability to detect effects
  • Sample size → how much data we collect

Power analysis is where all of these come together.

BEAN is not just a set of concepts—it is a system of tradeoffs.

There is no single “correct” choice

One of the most important takeaways from this chapter is that there is rarely a single correct answer when designing a study.

Instead, researchers must balance competing priorities:

  • Want to detect small effects? You need a larger sample
  • Want to reduce false positives? You may need a lower alpha, which reduces power
  • Have a limited sample size? You may only be able to detect larger effects

These are not problems—they are the reality of research design.

Thinking in tradeoffs

Let’s make this concrete.

Imagine you expect a small effect but can only recruit a small sample.

You now face a decision:

  • Increase your sample size (if possible)
  • Accept lower power (risk missing the effect)
  • Adjust your expectations about detectable effects

Similarly, if you lower alpha to be more conservative:

  • You reduce false positives
  • But you also make it harder to detect real effects

Every decision you make in BEAN affects the others.

From statistics to research design

Up to this point, much of statistics may have felt like:

  • calculating values
  • running tests
  • interpreting outputs

Power analysis shifts the focus to:

designing studies that are capable of answering your research question.

Instead of asking:

“Is this result significant?”

We start asking:

  • Was the study capable of detecting the effect?
  • Was the sample size sufficient?
  • Were the design choices justified?

Final takeaway

If you remember one thing from this chapter, let it be this:

Statistical significance is not just about the data you collect—it is about the decisions you make before you collect the data.

BEAN provides a framework for making those decisions thoughtfully.

Looking ahead

In the next chapters, we will return to specific statistical tests. As you learn those tests, keep BEAN in mind:

  • What effect size is being tested?
  • What assumptions are being made?
  • How much power does the study likely have?

These questions will help you move beyond simply running analyses to truly understanding and evaluating research.
